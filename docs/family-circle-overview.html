Core Architecture

The Rings Model

Coordination load distributes by relationship and role. As you move outward, participation weight decreases. Privacy stays intact — outer rings never see what center ring sees.

Center Ring
Primary household, kids
Full calendar, all events, manages the system and all dependencies
High involvement
First Ring
Co-parent, ex-spouse, regular grandparents
Relevant events only — their custody days, their responsibilities
Consulted, not managing
Second Ring
Babysitters, tutors, coaches
Only what's needed for their role — pickup time, location, notes
Specific purpose only
Outer Ring
Family friends, extended family, occasional helpers
Minimal — just what they need when you ask
Minimal, on-demand

The Core Engine

🔍
"Can I do Thursday 6–8pm?"

Custody Check

Event Check

Do any of my kids have activities during that window?

3

If my kids are elsewhere, who's responsible? Are they covered?

4

If something goes wrong, who in my circle is available?

Build Sequence

Phase Approach

Phase 1 builds the coordination engine. Phase 2 adds connectivity — but only after logistics work reliably first.

Phase 1 — Build First

Solve Logistics

  • Calendar input and event management
  • Conflict detection and dependency verification
  • Decision engine (the core query model)
  • Onboarding and circle management
  • SMS adoption for outer rings
Phase 2 — After Logistics Work

Add Connectivity

  • In-app messaging and coordinated help requests
  • Photo and event sharing from activities
  • Family circle engagement layer
Not built until Phase 1 is solid. This is optional social glue on top of working coordination — not the other way around.
Design Principle
Primary simplicity.
The system's complexity is invisible.
The person who downloads the app thinks about two things: people and schedule. Everything else — SMS infrastructure, parsing, conflict detection, dependency resolution, review queues — is the system's invisible job. No technical setup. No learning curve.
