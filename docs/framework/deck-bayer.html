The starting point

Your team already has
what AI can never create.

Every organization carries knowledge that no AI tool can generate — the years of domain expertise, process nuance, and hard-won experience embedded in the people who do the work every day. It's the person who knows why an approval step exists, not just that it does. The one who can recognize a data issue in seconds because they've encountered it dozens of times before. AI cannot create that context or judgment on its own.

Tools like Claude or Cursor can dramatically amplify this knowledge. The real unlock happens when domain experts can reliably express that knowledge through AI in a repeatable, aligned way — turning individual expertise into scalable capability, without changing how the business fundamentally operates.

The other side of the equation

When the tool isn't enough on its own.

These tools excel at producing outputs, but they don't define how work should move. There's no shared backbone that enforces quality, no built-in progression from idea to implementation, and no common checkpoints where decisions are validated before moving forward. Each effort becomes a one-off, shaped by whoever happens to be driving in the moment.

The result feels powerful but fragile. Teams move faster, yet without guardrails that maintain alignment or make progress legible to others. Over time, that fragility compounds — work diverges, context disappears, and confidence erodes. That missing structure is the gap. Not in capability, but in everything required to make that capability dependable at scale.

What it comes down to

Structure. Visibility.
Confidence.

Six variables. Three words. Every one of the six comes back to these — and every team building with AI right now is missing at least one of them.

Structure

Structure that starts before the first line is written, guides every decision along the way, and leaves a record when the work is done.

In the environment — pre-established tools, components, and standards so no builder ever starts from scratch. The guardrails exist before anyone opens a project. In the process — defined phases, explicit gates, and a clear bar for "done" agreed before build and verified after. In the knowledge — intent, decisions, and rationale captured as the work progresses, so the "why" never lives in just one person's head.

Visibility

Visibility that makes the state of every project readable to every person on the team — at any moment, without asking.

In how you engage — the builder sees the criteria, the PM sees the intent and outcomes, the stakeholder sees what's done, what's in flight, and what's next. In the artifacts — everything the work produces is in plain language anyone can read, regardless of technical background. And over time — decisions made weeks ago are still readable, because the "why" travels with the work, not with the person who made the call.

Confidence

Confidence is what's left when structure and visibility are working — the certainty that what was built is what was meant, that it meets the bar, and that anyone who touches it next will understand why.

It's not a feature you can point to. It's the feeling that accumulates on the other side of both. The engineer confident the work meets the bar before it ships. The PM confident the AI built what was actually asked for. The person who picks this up six months from now confident they can understand it — because the record is all there.

How it works

Every phase has a purpose,
an output, and a gate.

The framework is the method behind the model. Claude is the capability — what it can do. The framework is the method — how it applies that capability here, in a way that reflects your process rather than its defaults.

Think of it like a talented new hire. On day one, their output reflects their own assumptions, instincts, and shortcuts. Hand them your company's processes, standards, and institutional knowledge, and their output starts to reflect yours instead. The framework is what you hand Claude before it starts. Without it, you get Claude's defaults. With it, you get your process — reliably applied, every time.

What the work produces

Every phase leaves
something behind.

The framework doesn't treat documentation as a separate step. It produces a structured record as the natural output of how work moves — intent, decisions, criteria, and results maintained alongside every unit of work from first conversation to deploy.

Work moves in small, defined pieces with clear checkpoints between them. Each phase checks in before moving forward — so you adjust course early, not after months of build. Nothing accumulates in isolation. Nothing gets handed off as a surprise. That's what keeps the work agile. Without it, AI-assisted building defaults to the opposite — one person builds in isolation, and the handoff is where the problems surface.

Product brief
The intent, the problem, the audience. Established in Discovery. Referenced at every gate so the work never drifts from what it was meant to solve.
Done criteria
Plain language definition of what finished means for every unit of work. Defined before build starts. Verified before it closes. The bar doesn't move.
Decision log
Why choices were made, what was considered, what was ruled out. The context that usually lives in one person's head — captured as the work progresses, not reconstructed after.
Phase outputs
Each phase hands something concrete to the next. Design hands approved specs to Plan. Plan hands a structured backlog to Build. No gaps, no assumptions.
Quality contract
The specific bar each unit of work has to meet — checked before the work closes. Not a final review. A condition of moving forward.
Handoff record
Everything the next person needs to pick up where you left off. Exists before anyone asks for it — because it was built alongside the work, not written after.
