What This Phase Is

Brainstorm is the optional first phase of the Solo Builder Framework — the right entry point when you have something rattling around in your head but can't yet articulate it clearly enough for Discovery to be productive. It's a conversation, not a ceremony. There's no form to fill in, no requirements to gather, no architecture to consider. The core question is simple: do we understand this idea well enough to tell its story?

What makes the brainstorm in the framework different from a freeform chat is that it has structure underneath — a backbone that ensures nothing important gets missed while staying invisible in the actual conversation. The brainstorm moves through the idea itself, who it's for, where the value moment lives, and what the product actually does.

Midway through the session — once the idea has enough shape — a rough visual sketch appears. This is not the design sprint output. It's a thinking tool: a single low-fidelity HTML screen showing approximately what the moment of value looks like. It gives the conversation something to react to. Before the visual sketch, a preliminary process sketch captures the rough as-is and to-be process. Both sketches are thinking tools that make the conversation sharper without turning it into a design session.

The brainstorm also does routing. Feasibility unknowns get flagged and sent to Research rather than pursued mid-session. Ideas that turn out to be multiple independent things get identified for decomposition. The brainstorm ends when the core idea is clear enough to hand off to Discovery — or when it's clear more time is needed.

Step-by-Step Experience

What actually happens in a brainstorm session.

1 · You bring the idea in your own words

There's no format required. You describe what's on your mind — a problem you've noticed, something frustrating that shouldn't be that hard, a capability you think should exist. The skill listens first, doesn't redirect, doesn't start refining. It asks one good question to go deeper, following your thread rather than jumping to the next item on a list.

This is intentional. The way you describe the idea in your own words often contains the most important clue — what you're actually frustrated by, who the real person in the story is, where the value moment really lives. Redirecting too early loses that signal.

2 · The conversation develops the idea across four threads

The backbone of the brainstorm is four threads: the idea itself (what's underneath, not the features), who it's for (a real person in a real situation, not a user type), the value moment (when does this actually become useful to them?), and what's in the idea (the capabilities — held lightly, not locked down). The conversation moves between these naturally, going where it needs to go.

The skill volunteers thoughts when it has them — patterns that work or don't work, something in the idea that doesn't add up, a direction worth questioning. It pushes back when something is unclear. This is not a passive interview.

3 · Feasibility unknowns get flagged, not pursued

When something in the conversation raises a "can this actually work" question — an AI component, a data integration, a real-time requirement — it gets captured explicitly rather than explored mid-session. The skill names the research question precisely: not "we should look into that" but "before we can design this component, we need to know X — that's the research question."

These items don't kill the idea. They get parked in a Research queue and the brainstorm continues.

4 · The preliminary process sketch surfaces early

Before the visual sketch, when the conversation starts describing how something works — "currently someone has to manually do X and then email Y" — the process sketch activates. It captures the rough as-is (how the process works today, even if it's manual and informal) and the beginning of a to-be (what it would look like if this idea succeeded). This isn't the formal process map that comes out of Discover; it's a thinking tool that makes the brainstorm sharper.

5 · A rough sketch appears mid-session

When the conversation has enough shape — there's a clear sense of who this is for and what the value moment looks like — a single rough HTML screen appears. Intentionally low fidelity: structure and content only, no polish. It's not a design sprint artifact. It's the equivalent of someone walking to the whiteboard mid-conversation and drawing something. The point is to give the conversation something to react to.

After the sketch, the question is "warmer or colder?" One or two rounds of reaction is enough. This is not the design sprint — if it starts feeling like one, the brainstorm pulls back.

6 · The session ends with explicit routing

The brainstorm doesn't fade out — it ends with a clear statement of what's going where and why. Core idea ready? Hand off to Discover. Feasibility questions still open? Name the research items and route them to Research before Discovery begins. Idea turned out to be multiple independent things? Name the pieces and brainstorm the first piece separately.

The output is a brief document — not a spec — written as a story: who the idea is for, what they do, where the value moment is. Good enough that a designer could start sketching from it.

What Can You Brainstorm?

Five concrete examples — how the framework engages with each.

IdeaWhat the brainstorm surfacesWhat becomes clearer
Freelancer invoicing tool
"I hate chasing clients for payment"		 The frustration isn't sending invoices — it's the follow-up. The real problem is visibility and leverage after the invoice is sent. Who it's for becomes specific: a solo consultant with 5–10 active clients. The value moment is when a client is 5 days past due and the freelancer knows exactly what to say and has a one-click way to send it. Automated reminder cadence gets flagged as a research item.
Neighborhood group buy coordinator
"What if neighbors could pool orders from local farms?"		 The idea is really about making the organizer role sustainable so it doesn't burn out. The recipient isn't just "neighbors" — it's the one person willing to run the thing. The process sketch surfaces the coordination loop: farm posts → organizer shares → neighbors commit → organizer aggregates → order placed. Payment collection is a research item.
Gym class scheduling for small studios
"Boutique gyms can't afford Mindbody"		 A small studio running 10 classes a week doesn't need a POS system. They need: post a schedule, let people sign up, send a reminder, manage a waitlist. That's the whole job. The value moment is Saturday morning when the owner knows whether a class is full and who's on the waitlist. Member management is a separate idea — it shouldn't be in Phase 1.
Personal reading tracker
"Goodreads but without the social noise"		 The person wants a private record of what they've read and something that helps them decide what to read next without scrolling recommendations from strangers. The recommendation component gets flagged as a research item immediately. The brainstorm focuses the core on the reading log — which is complete and useful even before recommendations exist.
Shift handoff tool for restaurants
"The paper log never gets read"		 The paper log fails because nobody reads it before their shift, and it doesn't surface what's actually urgent. What people actually need is a 60-second brief when they clock in. The process sketch makes the handoff loop visible. The clock-in trigger surfaces a question: integration with the POS/time-tracking system, or standalone? Flagged as a research item.

The Gate — Brainstorm to Discover

The gate from Brainstorm to Discover is a judgment call, not a checklist. The question is: is the idea clear enough for a productive Discovery conversation? That means you understand who it's for, roughly what they do, and where the value moment is. It doesn't mean the idea is fully resolved — Discovery is where it gets fully articulated. It means there's enough to start the narrative.

The brainstorm ends explicitly. The skill makes a routing call and states it plainly: "The core idea is clear enough for Discovery — here's what we understand and what remains open." Or: "There's still a feasibility question that should get answered before Discovery — here's the research item." The session doesn't just trail off.

Handoff to Discover

What you bring into the Discovery conversation.

What transfersHow it's used in Discover
The idea direction The story of who this is for, what they do, and where the value moment lives. Becomes the opening context for the Discovery conversation so it doesn't start from scratch. Discovery builds on this rather than re-deriving it.
The preliminary process sketch The rough as-is and to-be from the brainstorm session. Discovery hands this to process-mapper to formalize into the actual as-is and to-be process maps. Having a starting sketch means the process conversation in Discovery is refinement, not derivation from zero.
Decisions already made Anything that shaped the direction of the idea during the brainstorm — decomposition decisions (what's in Phase 1 vs. later), scope boundaries, framing choices. These don't get re-litigated in Discovery. They're treated as agreed and carried forward.
Open questions Discovery needs to answer The things the brainstorm surfaced but deliberately didn't resolve — questions that belong in the Discovery narrative conversation rather than the brainstorm. Examples: the full use case from beginning to end, the edge cases that matter, the definition of done for the first version.
Research items, if any Feasibility unknowns that need answers before the design can proceed. If these are blocking, Research comes before Discovery. If they're non-blocking, they get held and addressed before the relevant design decisions are made.

When to Skip Brainstorm

Brainstorm is for fuzzy ideas. If you already know what you want to build — you can describe who it's for, what they do, and where the value moment is — skip this and go straight to Discover. Brainstorming a fully-formed idea wastes time and can actually muddy clarity you already have.

The start skill makes this routing decision for you. It reads what you bring and routes to Brainstorm if the idea needs exploration, or directly to Discover if it's ready. You don't have to decide — just describe what you want to work on.

The clearest signals that you should skip Brainstorm: You can describe in one sentence who the product is for and what the moment of value looks like. · You've already built something like this before and you know the shape of it. · You have prior work — notes, a PRD, a design, a working prototype — that defines the idea. Use the onboard path to bring that into the framework instead. · You feel resistance at the idea of spending time exploring — you want to build, not think. That's valid signal. Go to Discover and let the narrative conversation surface what's unclear.

The framework is agile, not waterfall. Brainstorm isn't a phase you have to complete before you're allowed to proceed. It's a tool for a specific situation. Use it when it helps. Skip it when it doesn't.
