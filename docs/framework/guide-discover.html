Solo Builder Framework — Phase Guide

Discover + Tech Context

The foundation everything else builds from. Discover surfaces the full story before any design decisions are made. Tech Context locks the stack before any screens are drawn. Together they prevent the most expensive mistakes in the framework.

What This Phase Is

Phase flow: Brainstorm (optional) → Discover → Tech Context → Design Sprint. Both gates must pass before Design Sprint begins.

Discover is the day-one conversation. It is not requirements gathering and it is not a scoping session. It is the conversation a good engineer or UX person has before they touch a keyboard — the one where they listen, ask the right questions, and build a shared picture of what you're actually trying to create. By the end, everyone would tell the same story. That story feeds the design sprint, not an engineer's backlog.

The four zones of the Discover conversation — who, the journey, the moment it matters, and what done looks like — surface the full use case from beginning to end. Each zone is explored at a depth calibrated to the complexity of what you're building.

Tech Context runs immediately after Discover, before Design Sprint begins. It establishes what you're building on and what constraints that creates — platform, stack, architecture rules, engineering principles. This matters because every downstream decision is technically constrained: the design can only show what the stack can produce, the data scaffold is tied to the data layer, the plan's done criteria are written in terms of the framework in use. A design sprint that runs without tech context produces screens that may be unbuilable on the actual platform.

Together these two phases are the foundation. If either is skipped or incomplete, every phase downstream either makes wrong decisions or has to backtrack. The gates exist precisely because of this — there is no way into Design Sprint without them both.

Skills in This Phase

discover — Phase Skill

Conversational discovery that builds a shared understanding of what you're trying to create — beginning to end. Not requirements gathering. Not a spec. A story-based brief that feeds the design sprint. The core question: "Do we understand this well enough to make something that shows what it could be?"

AspectDetail
What it does Receives your high-level overview without interruption. Confirms shared understanding with one or two clarity questions. Conducts the narrative conversation across four zones. Asks clarifying questions to complete the as-is picture for process-mapper. Presents and validates the as-is and to-be process maps. Produces the discovery brief as a short narrative document. Ends with the design on-ramp question to route what comes next.
Zone 1 — Who Not a demographic. A real picture of the person using this, their context when they arrive, what triggered them to come here, and what they already know versus what they don't. "Who is this person and what's their context when they use this?" — "What are they doing before they open this?"
Zone 2 — The Journey Walk through every step from open to close. No skipping ahead. "Walk me through what someone does from the moment they open it." — "And then what happens?" One or two questions per round — never a barrage.
Zone 3 — The Moment It Matters Where this becomes genuinely useful. The payoff moment. "Where does this actually become valuable to them?" — "What's the thing they'd tell someone else about?" This often becomes the hero screen.
Zone 4 — What Done Looks Like Outcomes, not features. "When they're done and it worked — what did they accomplish?" — "What would make them come back?" Grounds the design in real outcomes.
Outputs docs/discovery-brief.md · docs/process/as-is-[name].md · docs/process/to-be-[name].md · Design on-ramp decision (Figma / reference / from scratch) documented at bottom of brief

tech-context — Phase Skill

Establishes the technical stack, architecture constraints, and engineering principles before design review, planning, or build. Created once per project — never recreated, only updated when the stack changes. Referenced by every downstream skill for the life of the project.

AspectDetail
Two routing paths Known profile — organizational project with an established stack. Two questions: org or solo? Which platform? Loads profile, confirms key constraints, produces the doc. General solo — no established profile. Asks questions one at a time: deployment target, frontend, backend/data, component library, API communication, auth, hosting, engineering principles. Either path ends with a summary confirmation before writing the document.
What it captures Full stack table: frontend, component library, API comms, backend, auth, database, deployment, CI/CD, linting, testing. Architecture constraints — things design and build must account for beyond the stack. Engineering principles — standards and rules for this project. Branching and delivery model. Infrastructure slices — prerequisites that must be Done before any feature slice starts; identified here, not in Design Review. Secrets and config management pattern.
Downstream impact Design Sprint — architecture constraints shape what can be designed. Data Scaffold — stack determines mock data format and data layer pattern. Plan — done criteria written with real technical specificity. Build — the build bible. Stack, principles, branching, secrets — all here.

process-mapper — Always-On (piloted / guided)

Activates during Discover in piloted and guided modes. Documents the as-is process and produces the agreed to-be map. The to-be map is the process contract — its agreed structure does not change without an explicit decision.

What it does in this phaseThe process contract
Documents the as-is process exactly as described — including manual steps, workarounds, and exceptions outside any system. Presents as-is for validation before proceeding. Drafts the to-be map from the journey conversation — every step, every decision point, every outcome. Presents to-be for agreement before Design Sprint begins. Produces both maps as markdown files in docs/process/. The to-be map agreed here is the contract that every downstream skill works against. Design Sprint cross-references screens against the to-be map — gaps flagged and decided. Design Review requires a process anchor on every slice before it reaches Ready. Phase Test validates that what was built matches the to-be map intent. The map is updated as screens and slices are added — but its agreed structure does not change without an explicit decision.

Step-by-Step Experience

1 · You give the high-level overview

Discover

Describe what you want to build at a high level. The discover skill does not interrupt it or redirect it. It lets you tell it your way. After you finish, it scans for who this is for, what problem or opportunity it addresses, and any existing context in the project.

2 · Clarity check (one round, at most)

Discover

One or two questions at most to confirm shared understanding before going deeper. The right kind: "When you say [X], do you mean [A] or [B]?" — not challenges, not premise questions. If the overview was clear, this step is skipped and the conversation moves directly to the zones.

3 · Narrative conversation — Zones 1 and 2

Discover

Zone 1 — Who: Not a demographic. A real picture of the person using this, their context when they arrive, what triggered them to come here, and what they already know versus what they don't.

Zone 2 — The Journey: Walk through every step from open to close; no skipping ahead. One or two questions per round — never a barrage. If an answer is vague, go one level deeper before moving on.

4 · Narrative conversation — Zones 3 and 4

Discover

Zone 3 — The Moment It Matters: Where this becomes genuinely useful. "What's the thing they'd tell someone else about?" This often becomes the hero screen.

Zone 4 — What Done Looks Like: Outcomes, not features. "When they're done and it worked — what did they accomplish?" Grounds the design in real outcomes.

Depth is calibrated: simple use case = 3–5 questions total; moderate complexity = 5–10; vague greenfield = multi-round conversation. If you say "that's enough" — the skill respects it, notes where clarity is thin, and proceeds.

5 · As-is and to-be process maps

process-mapper

Before writing the discovery brief, process-mapper activates. It asks any remaining clarifying questions to document how the process works today — including manual steps, workarounds, and exceptions that happen outside any system. The as-is may be entirely offline: emails, spreadsheets, phone calls, decisions in someone's head.

The as-is map is presented for validation. Once confirmed, the to-be map is drafted from the journey conversation and presented for explicit agreement. This agreement is the process contract. Both maps are saved to docs/process/.

6 · Discovery brief written + design on-ramp decided

Discover

A short narrative document — not a spec, not bullet-point requirements. The story, key moments in the journey, open threads that don't need resolving before design starts, and what's explicitly out of scope. Small on purpose: a good designer doesn't need 40 pages to start sketching.

Then one final question: "Do you have an existing Figma file, a product you'd like to reference for look and feel, or are we starting from scratch?" The answer is documented at the bottom of the brief and routes the design sprint.

7 · Stack established via profile or questions

tech-context

Two paths. If this is an organizational project, a profile is loaded and key constraints are confirmed. If it's a general solo project, questions are asked one at a time. Never asked all at once.

When enough is known, the stack is summarized for confirmation: "Based on what you've shared: [summary]. Before I write this up — anything else that would constrain how we build?"

8 · Infrastructure slices identified

tech-context

As part of writing docs/tech-context.md, infrastructure slices are called out — these are the prerequisite slices that must be Done before any feature slice can start. They are identified from the tech stack and platform constraints now, not later during Design Review. A project on a module platform that calls an existing API has infrastructure slices. A greenfield solo project starting with a mock data layer has infrastructure slices. These go directly into the backlog when Design Review begins.

Handoff to Design Sprint

Three documents. Three distinct purposes. All required.

DocumentHow Design Sprint uses it
discovery-brief.md
The story		 Reads as the narrative foundation. The story gives the design sprint context for every decision: what screen matters most, what the payoff moment looks like, what's out of scope. Zone 3 (the moment it matters) is the hero screen candidate — the design sprint opens here. The design on-ramp at the bottom determines the starting point: from scratch, Figma import, or reference-based.
to-be-[name].md
What to build against		 Process-mapper cross-references every screen in the sprint against the to-be map — steps not covered by any screen are surfaced as gaps for you to decide on. Annotates the to-be map with screen references as screens are approved. The to-be map is the process contract — no step is designed around without a decision.
tech-context.md
Aesthetic + stack constraints		 Reads for what can and cannot be designed. Module constraint = no login screen, no global nav in the designs. Component library = design uses those components, not custom ones. Mobile target = layout decisions are made for that form factor from frame one.
Design Sprint stops if either gate is missing. The discovery brief is how the sprint knows what to design. The tech context is how it knows what it can design. Without both, the artifact produced may be a complete mismatch with what you can actually build.

What to Watch For

IssueWhy it mattersWhat to do instead
To-be map agreed too quickly — the map was drafted and you said "looks fine" without real scrutiny of whether it covers all the decision branches you described in the journey. This is the process contract. Every downstream decision — screen design, slice definition, done criteria, phase test — is anchored to it. A vague or incomplete to-be map produces slice boundaries that don't match the real process, which surfaces as confusion in Design Review and rework in Build. Go back to Zone 2. Walk the journey again step by step. Confirm that every step you described is represented in the to-be map. Check: are there branches? Edge cases you mentioned? Multiple user types doing different things? The map must cover them.
Tech Context skipped or deferred — "we'll figure out the stack later" or "let's just get into design first." Downstream skills cannot route correctly. The design sprint may produce screens for a mobile app when you're building a web module. The data scaffold defaults to the wrong data layer. Infrastructure slices are never identified — and feature work begins with no foundation underneath it. Run tech-context before opening design-sprint. It takes 5–10 minutes for a solo project and less for a known organizational profile. This is not optional overhead — it is the routing mechanism for everything that follows.
Discovery brief too vague to produce a hero screen — the story section is abstract, describes a type of product without a clear picture of who uses it. Zone 3 (the moment it matters) is supposed to surface the hero screen candidate. If the brief doesn't have a specific payoff moment, the design sprint opens without a starting target. Screens get designed that nobody can evaluate against the brief. Before writing the brief, check: can you name the specific moment in the journey where this becomes genuinely useful? If not, go deeper in Zone 3. If you still can't name it, the Zone 2 journey may not be specific enough — go back and sharpen it first.
As-is process skipped because "there is no current process" — assuming the process doesn't exist because there's no system today. There is always an as-is process. It may be entirely manual — an email chain, a spreadsheet, a phone call. Skipping it means the to-be map is designed without accounting for the manual workarounds and exception-handling steps that the new product must replace or improve on. Ask specifically: "How does this happen today — even if there's no system?" and "What's the most common place this goes wrong today?" Then document exactly what was described. Even "someone sends an email and waits" is a valid as-is step.
Infrastructure slices not identified during Tech Context — tech-context is completed but the infrastructure slices table is left empty or marked TBD. Infrastructure slices are the prerequisites before any feature slice can start — authentication setup, API client wiring, database schema, environment configuration. If they're not identified here, feature slices go into the backlog without a foundation to build on. When producing tech-context.md, ask: "What must exist before a single feature slice can start?" For a React app: Can the project boot? Is the component library installed? Is auth wired? These become infrastructure slices in the backlog.
